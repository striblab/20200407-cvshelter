Three charts tracking how Minnesotans are socially distancing

By Jeff Hargarten
Star Tribune Updated April 7, 2020

Minnesota life has largely ground to a halt since Gov. Tim Walz announced a shelter-in-place order in late March, the strongest tactic so far combating mounting death tolls from COVID-19 infections across the state.

Signs suggest these measures are apparently working to slow the coronavirus pandemic in Minnesota, and various data points show Minnesotans take this order seriously enough to curb their normal habits in dramatic ways.

With federal social distancing guidelines in place until at least May and many summer events being canceled entirely, here’s a deeper look at some ways Minnesotans have quarantined themselves against COVID-19:

Minnesota roads are less crowded

Fewer have been traveling from one point to another on Minnesota roads, according to Minnesota Department of Transportation data.

Comparing daily traffic volume this year to corresponding days in 2019 shows Minnesota's roads are half as busy during the quarantine.

Even before the shelter-in-place order took effect on March 28, Minnesotans were commuting far less than usual as employees shifted to working from home, public events canceled and businesses reduced service or shuttered entirely in response to coronavirus spreading throughout the community.

Phone tracking shows Minnesotans are homebound

Some organizations have started using cellphone tracking data to measure how people are social distancing, including Cuebiq, Unacast and Google.

All reveal Minnesotans are venturing outside their homes significantly less than average, and Google's metrics show the impact of transit schedule limitations, closed retail stores and changes to work routines.

Sheltering efforts vary by location

Unacast cellphone tracking suggests some Minnesotans could be better about quarantining, as the state's assigned social distancing grade slides from an A- just after the shelter-in-place order to a C by April.

Hennepin and Ramsey counties received the best grades in the metro area, while Anoka scored the poorest for staying home.

Some of the harsher scores assigned to greater Minnesota might be skewed by those residents typically traveling further for essential trips to stores or work. Analyses from both Google and Descartes Labs, for instance, shows significant decreases in mobility among Minnesotans in rural counties.
